# The People Deciding AI Can Do Your Job Have Never Done Your Job
**From:** Slow AI  <theslowai@substack.com>
**Date:** Wed, 11 Mar 2026 09:02:11 +0000
**ID:** 19cdc27d369ae0fd

---

View this post on the web at https://theslowai.substack.com/p/the-people-deciding-ai-can-do-your

Jack Dorsey cut Block’s workforce from 10,000 to under 6,000 and called it an AI transformation.
A former head of communications at Square [ https://substack.com/redirect/92141caf-a40a-48b0-8a79-2148186110c0?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] looked at the actual cuts: the policy team shrunk, the diversity and inclusion roles disappeared, the headcount that had ballooned from 4,000 to 13,000 during pandemic hiring was corrected. 
As one analyst observed: 
“The vast majority of these cuts were probably not due to AI.”
A former employee put it more bluntly: 
“It’s organisational bloat wearing an AI costume.”
Block’s stock rose 17%.
In this post I will:
Show that companies are cutting jobs in anticipation of AI’s impact, not because AI is actually doing the work.
Argue that the people making these decisions are structurally unable to see what they are destroying.
Give paid subscribers a framework for mapping which parts of their own role are genuinely augmentable and which are being misidentified by people who have never done the work.
The anticipation economy
A Harvard Business Review study [ https://substack.com/redirect/83a4bcef-ce6f-4974-a65b-dda86f87d2d5?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] of 1,006 global executives found that 60% of organisations have already reduced headcount in anticipation of AI’s future capabilities. Not in response to demonstrated performance. In anticipation.
Only 2% made reductions related to actual AI implementation.
Read that again. 60% are cutting. 2% have evidence it works.
The same study found that 44% of executives consider generative AI the hardest technology to evaluate economically. They cannot measure its value. They are firing people anyway.
This pattern has a name: AI displacement risk.
Anthropic’s own researchers [ https://substack.com/redirect/6e14b528-565f-475c-b193-b9838fc66e88?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] call it the gap between ‘theoretical capability’ and ‘observed exposure.’ Computer and maths workers face 94% theoretical AI capability but Claude currently covers just 33% of these tasks. The gap between what AI could do in a demo and what it does in a job is enormous. 
The people making the decisions see the demo. The people losing their jobs know the difference.
The view from the boardroom versus the view from the desk
Geoffrey Hinton predicted in 2016 that AI would outperform radiologists [ https://substack.com/redirect/83a4bcef-ce6f-4974-a65b-dda86f87d2d5?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] within five years. A decade later, no radiologist has lost their job to AI. The reason is simple: reading an image is one task within a role that involves hundreds. A job is not a task list. It is a web of judgement calls, context, relationships, and institutional knowledge that no flowchart captures and no executive summary conveys.
A CIO survey [ https://substack.com/redirect/a89befef-c09d-4213-9f0c-cdb9100450ac?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] published this month found that even managers resist the replacement narrative. 62% say AI cannot create new products or services that customers will want. 53% report that customers prefer working with humans. These are not Luddites. These are the people one level closer to the work than the C-suite, and they can already see what the boardroom cannot.
The pattern is always the same. The further you are from the work, the more replaceable it looks.
What a job actually contains
When Klarna’s CEO replaced customer service staff with AI, he celebrated the cost savings. Then the quality dropped. Then they had recruitment drive for human employees [ https://substack.com/redirect/e96030bf-58b9-47d5-ba17-8208303f2a33?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. This will happen more and more.
A job contains things that are visible from above and things that are not. The visible parts are tasks: answering emails, writing reports, summarising data. These are what AI demos replicate. The invisible parts are everything else: knowing which email needs a careful tone, knowing which report will be ignored and which will trigger a restructure, knowing that the data is misleading because you were in the room when it was collected.
Institutional knowledge. Relational judgement. Contextual awareness. None of these appear in a job description. All of them disappear when the person leaves.
The executives making the cuts do not see these things because they do not do the work. They see inputs and outputs. They do not see the connective tissue that holds it together.
Why this matters for you
Anthropic’s research warns of a potential ‘Great Recession for white-collar workers’ if unemployment doubles in AI-exposed occupations. The most at-risk group is 16 % more likely to be female, earns 47% more than average, and is nearly four times as likely to hold a graduate degree.
This is not a blue-collar displacement story replaying in new clothes. This is a new pattern: the most educated and highest-paid workers being cut by executives who cannot evaluate whether the replacement technology works, because 44% of them admit they cannot measure its value.

# The Models You Can Inspect Are the Ones Being Banned
**From:** Slow AI  <theslowai@substack.com>
**Date:** Tue, 10 Mar 2026 09:01:11 +0000
**ID:** 19cd6fb069e02688

---

View this post on the web at https://theslowai.substack.com/p/the-models-you-can-inspect-are-the

DeepSeek has been banned. OpenAI just signed a deal with the Pentagon. The models you can inspect are the ones being blocked.
In this post we will:
Slow AI  is a reader-supported publication. To receive new posts and support my work, consider becoming a free or paid subscriber.
Look at what OpenAI is doing with the Pentagon while lobbying to ban its Chinese competitors.
Compare the data sovereignty of open source Chinese models with closed American ones.
Ask why familiarity, not risk, is driving institutional AI procurement decisions.
DeepSeek [ https://substack.com/redirect/dc41fb96-6990-4a51-abb6-51416b92f568?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] (a Chinese AI lab that builds open source language models comparable to GPT-4) has been banned in Italy, Australia, South Korea, Taiwan, and across US government agencies. Universities have blocked it from institutional networks. The Group of Eight research universities in Australia removed it entirely [ https://substack.com/redirect/7cd5b7ca-07a0-45ca-b048-978d27b6b09c?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. George Mason University prohibited it on all devices [ https://substack.com/redirect/08803a04-6fa2-49b1-8271-acbbbed9f259?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ].
The reason, every time, is the same. China.
China’s intelligence laws require companies to cooperate with state agencies. DeepSeek stores user data on servers governed by Chinese law. Feroot Security found encrypted code in DeepSeek’s web login linked to China Mobile [ https://substack.com/redirect/4df74ddc-77d0-4ca4-bafc-70febd6e4c8b?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ], a company banned in the US for military ties. These are not trivial concerns.
But here is the question nobody is asking. Why does the same scrutiny not apply to the tools you are already using?
The Pentagon’s AI
OpenAI just signed a deal with the US Department of Defense [ https://substack.com/redirect/1bf6ce86-0da0-4f3a-aa53-b9a538c78eb8?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. Sam Altman admitted it “looked opportunistic and sloppy” [ https://substack.com/redirect/25092012-f7af-499c-9e66-a7179a761e42?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. The amended contract says the Pentagon will not use OpenAI for ‘mass domestic surveillance of US persons.’ That sentence exists because someone had to write it down.
Microsoft, OpenAI’s largest investor, has contracted with the Pentagon for decades. The Pentagon accessed OpenAI models through Microsoft Azure [ https://substack.com/redirect/9e7103f3-d2c9-49ab-a099-4a86f269d30b?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] before OpenAI had even lifted its own military use ban. The ban was performative. The access was real.
OpenAI has also lobbied the US government to ban DeepSeek and other Chinese open source models [ https://substack.com/redirect/c5997a68-3412-4ccf-bd76-62aae2561212?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] from allied nations, describing them as state-subsidised and state-controlled. A closed source company, backed by the US military’s longest-standing tech contractor, calling for bans on models you can actually inspect. Does something sound off here?
When Italy’s data protection authority asked DeepSeek about its data practices, DeepSeek said EU law did not apply to them. That was arrogant, and Italy banned them for it [ https://substack.com/redirect/8f7d1d94-3ddc-4cd0-ac53-ede69b1771c6?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. But when the same authority asked OpenAI about ChatGPT’s data practices in 2023, the response was not dramatically different. OpenAI was temporarily banned too [ https://substack.com/redirect/346d2d7f-a8ab-4651-8e89-b7a47a793a71?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. Most people have forgotten that.
The open source inversion
Here is where the argument turns inside out.
DeepSeek and Qwen (Alibaba’s open source AI model family) are open source. You can download the model weights. You can inspect the architecture. You can run them on your own hardware, on your own network, with your data never leaving your building. Qwen has been downloaded over 700 million times [ https://substack.com/redirect/e4b10f27-a321-4c0c-be85-1e5a8776e208?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. Airbnb, Perplexity, and Nvidia use Qwen models in production [ https://substack.com/redirect/0a148e8b-976e-4735-b098-079f7e185aa9?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. Chinese open source models now account for 30% of all AI downloads globally [ https://substack.com/redirect/2efb107e-c001-43de-bfa8-3ec1e2cc6f60?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ], overtaking the United States.
GPT-5 is closed. Claude is closed. You cannot see inside them. You cannot verify what they do with your data beyond reading a privacy policy written by lawyers whose job is to protect the company, not you. You send every prompt to servers in the United States, governed by US law, accessible to US intelligence agencies under FISA Section 702 [ https://substack.com/redirect/d851d588-0044-4c17-a763-890c6821fddb?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ].
The models you can inspect are the ones being banned. The models you cannot inspect are the ones your university just signed an enterprise deal for.
The University of Manchester has rolled out Microsoft 365 Copilot to all 65,000 students and staff [ https://substack.com/redirect/78b785d2-3458-46b0-817f-e3170943866a?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. The University of Oxford gives every student access to ChatGPT Edu [ https://substack.com/redirect/484ce174-0a3b-47f7-a2a2-61173258207c?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. These are presented as progress. Nobody called them a security risk.
Both sides of the same coin
Chinese AI models carry real risks. DeepSeek censors responses about Tiananmen Square, the Uyghur persecution, and Xi Jinping. Researchers in Estonia found DeepSeek concealing security information and inserting Chinese state narratives into answers [ https://substack.com/redirect/28ad44f7-284a-45da-9ba9-93d658cd68fb?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. These are not hypothetical dangers.
But American AI models carry risks too. OpenAI is now a for-profit company with military contracts. Microsoft embeds AI into operating systems that collect telemetry data at scale. Google trains models on your search history, your emails, your documents. Anthropic has taken over $8 billion from Amazon, whose cloud division holds contracts with the CIA and US intelligence agencies. The US government conducts mass surveillance programmes that would be illegal in most European countries.
The difference is familiarity. You trust American technology because you grew up with it. Because the interface is in English. Because the company has a .com address and a campus in San Francisco. Because the risk feels domestic rather than foreign, and domestic risks feel manageable even when they are not.
Where I stand (and where I am not sure)
I have used DeepSeek. I played around with it, tested it, compared outputs. And I noticed something uncomfortable about my own reaction: I was cautious in a way I have never been with ChatGPT, Gemini, or Claude. I treated it like a suspect. I did not extend the same suspicion to the tools I use every day.
I think I had been taken in. Not by propaganda, but by the quiet assumption that American technology is the default and everything else requires justification. I am a critical AI literacy researcher and I still caught myself doing it.
I do not have a clean answer here. I am genuinely unsure how much weight to give the data sovereignty concerns versus the open source advantages. I would love to hear from you. If you use DeepSeek or Qwen, tell me why. If you have chosen not to, tell me what convinced you. This is an open question, not a rhetorical one.
What the flags are hiding
None of this means DeepSeek is safe. It means safety was never the real sorting mechanism. Familiarity was.
Data sovereignty? Open source models let you keep everything on your own servers. The Chinese ones offer that. The American ones, mostly, do not. Censorship? Every model has it. GPT-5 filters through American cultural assumptions. DeepSeek filters through Chinese state policy. Neither is neutral. Neither tells you where the lines are drawn. Surveillance? Both governments have the will and the infrastructure to do it at scale. The mechanism differs. The outcome does not.
What to notice this week
Next time your organisation bans a Chinese AI model, check what it signed with an American one the same month. Read both contracts. Compare the data handling clauses. Notice which one required a security review and which one was waved through because the logo was familiar. The risk did not change. The flag did.

# The People Reviewing AI Research Are Using AI to Do It
**From:** Slow AI  <theslowai@substack.com>
**Date:** Sat, 7 Mar 2026 09:01:24 +0000
**ID:** 19cc788a66412ad0

---

View this post on the web at https://theslowai.substack.com/p/ai-peer-review-crisis-iclr

The world’s largest AI conference asked thousands of researchers to review each other’s work. One in five of those reviews was written entirely by AI. Nobody noticed until the system broke.
In this post I will:
Look at what happened when an API bug exposed reviewer identities for a major academic conference.
Examine the evidence that a fifth of peer reviews were AI-generated.
Ask what this means for anyone who still trusts academic peer review to separate good research from bad.
What happened at ICLR
ICLR [ https://substack.com/redirect/22ff3c13-c8eb-439e-a144-9922daae020f?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] is the International Conference on Learning Representations, one of the most prestigious venues in machine learning research. Getting a paper accepted there is a career-defining moment for many AI researchers. Its review process runs on OpenReview, a platform where submissions are evaluated anonymously by other researchers in the field. The anonymity is supposed to protect both sides: reviewers can be honest without fear of retaliation, and authors are judged on the work rather than the name.
On 27 November 2025, a bug in the OpenReview API exposed something that was supposed to stay hidden: the identities of authors, reviewers, and area chairs linked to paper submissions at ICLR 2026.
The bug may have been exploitable since 11 November [ https://substack.com/redirect/e54aa074-6175-412e-9af1-32c0aed106f5?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. By the time ICLR discovered it, malicious actors had already scraped data on over 10,000 papers and circulated it online. 
What followed was predictable. Third parties used the leaked data to threaten and bribe reviewers. Malicious comments identifying reviewers appeared on roughly 600 papers. ICLR confirmed [ https://substack.com/redirect/e54aa074-6175-412e-9af1-32c0aed106f5?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] the harassment, froze all review editing forms, deleted the comments, reverted scores, reassigned every paper to a new area chair, and banned the individual who circulated the names.
An entire review cycle, compromised in hours.
The reviews that reviewed themselves
Two weeks before the hack, a different problem surfaced.
Pangram Labs [ https://substack.com/redirect/90cfc55c-6f7d-42e7-84e2-65ec72333a6f?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] analysed all 19,000 papers and 70,000 reviews submitted to ICLR 2026. Their finding: 21% of reviews, roughly 15,899, were flagged as fully AI-generated. Over 50% showed some form of AI involvement.
The AI-generated reviews had distinctive characteristics. They were longer. They used section headers with bold formatting. They had low information density. One review ran to 3,000 words listing 40 weaknesses and 40 questions. That is what AI reviewing looks like: exhaustive and weightless.
Pangram also found that AI-generated reviews correlated with higher scores, despite lower paper quality. The reviewers were not using AI to sharpen their judgement. They were outsourcing it entirely.
A necessary caveat. Pangram Labs is a company that sells AI detection tools. They have a commercial interest in finding AI content. Their methodology claims a false positive rate of 1 in 10,000, validated against pre-2022 reviews from ArXiv and PubMed. Whether you trust the exact number or not, the pattern is clear. And ICLR’s own response [ https://substack.com/redirect/e0040bcd-b1c9-457c-aca9-c80f7553de1b?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] acknowledged the problem two weeks before Pangram published, announcing desk rejections for papers with undisclosed LLM usage and consequences for reviewers submitting ‘hallucinated reviews.’
Why this happened
ICLR submissions have nearly tripled in three years [ https://substack.com/redirect/2285572c-6745-4dff-8046-66f5e9570d10?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. In 2024, 7,304 papers were submitted. In 2025, that rose to 11,672. In 2026, 19,814.
To cope with that growth, ICLR recruited reviewers from the pool of submission authors. Undergraduates reviewing alongside professors. Each reviewer was assigned multiple papers on a tight deadline.
The system was designed for volume. AI-generated reviews are a rational response to an irrational workload.
This matters because the instinct is to blame the reviewers. They cheated. They were lazy. They took a shortcut. But the system that produced 19,814 submissions and then asked a stretched pool of volunteer reviewers to evaluate them in weeks is the system that made the shortcut inevitable. Individual integrity cannot compensate for structural impossibility.
The mirror
The field that builds AI cannot tell when AI is reviewing its own work. The tools they study are the tools undermining their evaluation process.
This is a trust infrastructure problem.
I see the same pattern in education [ https://substack.com/redirect/14da1c80-01f5-4327-9c29-1b0c4b608e61?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. Universities deploy AI detection tools to catch students using AI, while the detection itself is unreliable and the institutions themselves are adopting AI across their operations. Students get flagged. Educators do not. The asymmetry tells you everything about where the power sits.
A separate study [ https://substack.com/redirect/2637575b-79d7-4a0d-9185-36d11d99b180?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] examining AI-generated content in peer reviews across both ICLR and Nature Communications found minimal detection of AI content before 2022, followed by a sharp increase through 2025. Roughly 20% of ICLR reviews and 12% of Nature Communications reviews were classified as AI-generated by 2025. The trend is accelerating.
What peer review is actually for
I am the Chief Executive Editor of Geoscience Communication [ https://substack.com/redirect/6654aa53-6773-41dd-8331-da037533521d?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ], a peer-reviewed journal. I have published over 100 peer-reviewed papers [ https://substack.com/redirect/92108029-887e-4cbe-8cf2-144828b78a3a?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. I sit on the REF 2029 sub-panel for education [ https://substack.com/redirect/50de2f4e-5417-4927-b352-6176f11ed33a?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] that evaluates the quality of research outputs across UK universities. I say this not to credential myself but to be clear: I know what peer review is supposed to do and what it cannot survive.
Peer review works because a human reader brings their own knowledge, their own doubts, their own biases (acknowledged or not) to the text. An AI reviewer brings pattern matching. It can list 40 weaknesses because listing is what it does. It cannot tell you which of those 40 actually matters.
The word I keep coming back to is judgement. Not evaluation, not assessment, not scoring. Judgement. The willingness to say: this matters and that does not. The capacity to read between the lines of a methods section and sense that something is not quite right, even before you can articulate why. The experience that lets you distinguish a genuinely novel contribution from a well-formatted restatement of existing work.
An AI reviewer has none of this. It has fluency. It has structure. It has the appearance of thoroughness. What it lacks is the thing peer review exists to provide: an informed human mind engaging with another human mind’s work.
Why this matters for you
Even if you never submit to ICLR, you rely on peer-reviewed research. Your doctor does. Your government does. Your employer does. When a health policy cites ‘the evidence,’ that evidence was validated through peer review. When a drug is approved, peer-reviewed studies underpin the decision. When your child’s school adopts a new teaching method, someone somewhere pointed to a peer-reviewed paper.
If the system that validates research is itself contaminated by AI, the downstream effects touch everyone. Not immediately. Not dramatically. But steadily, as the gap between what peer review promises and what it delivers continues to widen.

Last week, Martian released Code Review Bench [ https://substack.com/redirect/b0e6d4da-f0b5-4302-b8e3-dfa04bfd4d9a?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] — the first independent, open-source benchmark for AI code review tools. It tracks over 200,000 real pull requests across GitHub, measures which review comments developers actually act on, and updates daily. The methodology and code are fully open source.
Kilo’s Code Reviewer ranked as the #1 open source code review tool across all values of beta. Whether you optimize for low noise (precision) or high thoroughness (recall), Kilo is the best open source option on the board.
Why This Benchmark is Different
If you’ve been around the AI benchmarking space, you know the pattern: a benchmark launches, tools optimize for it, it becomes less reliable. SWE-bench went through this cycle three times before OpenAI recommended everyone stop reporting scores entirely. Frontier models had memorized the gold patches. Over half the unsolved problems had broken tests.
Martian’s Code Review Bench was specifically built to avoid this. It works on two levels:
The offline benchmark runs every tool on the same set of 50 curated PRs with human-verified issues and scores them against a gold set. This gives you the controlled, apples-to-apples comparison.
The online benchmark is the reality check. It continuously collects data from code review tools operating in open source repos and tracks which comments developers actually fix versus ignore. When the offline rankings diverge from what’s happening in the wild, that’s the signal something is off.
The benchmark evaluates tools including CodeRabbit, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Gemini Code Assist, Augment, Qodo, Graphite, Baz, Greptile, and Kilo — among others. It measures precision (are the tool’s comments actually useful?) and recall (does the tool catch the real issues?), and uses an adjustable F-beta score so you can weight the tradeoff however you want.
Open Source Competing with Closed Source
This is the part we find most interesting.
The code review space has historically been dominated by closed-source, proprietary tools. Kilo’s Code Reviewer is open source. You can see how it works, configure it for your team’s priorities, and choose which model runs the review from 500+ options.
The fact that an open source tool is leading the OSS category on an independent benchmark — built by a research lab with no stake in who wins — is a signal. 
And this tracks with what we’ve been seeing in our own testing. When we tested three free models [ https://substack.com/redirect/2eaa8f11-4f6e-4109-9171-c144b898020c?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] on Kilo’s Code Reviewer last December, Grok Code Fast 1 caught all five planted security vulnerabilities and matched the detection rate of frontier models.

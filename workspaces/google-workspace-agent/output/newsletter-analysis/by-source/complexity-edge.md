Your most valuable AI skill is saying no
HereвҖҷs what happens in the moment of rejection. The person applies domain expertise the AI doesnвҖҷt have. They identify the specific gap between вҖңlooks rightвҖқ and вҖңis right.вҖқ And they articulate a constraint that didnвҖҷt exist as an explicit rule before they said it. A strategy partner sends back an AI-generated competitive analysis: вҖңWhereвҖҷs our proprietary insight on customer switching costs? Any firm with access to the same model could have produced this framing.вҖқ She just defined what separates her firmвҖҷs work from commodity output. A loan officer rejects a covenant tracking prototype: вҖңYou canвҖҷt treat a debt service coverage ratio the same as a minimum net worth requirement, they have completely different monitoring triggers.вҖқ He just wrote business logic that no requirements doc had captured. An editor kills a draft: вҖңThe thesis is buried in paragraph four. Lead with the provocation.вҖқ She just encoded an editorial standard.
Every one of those rejections is a knowledge-creation event. ThatвҖҷs the claim, and itвҖҷs the thing IвҖҷm not hearing anyone say. Not вҖңAI output needs human reviewвҖқ; everyone says that. The specific, non-obvious thing: the moment of rejection is the single most valuable moment in the entire AI workflow. Because the knowledge created in that moment, the constraint, the rule, the encoded taste, is the only part of the process that compounds.
And right now, all of it evaporates. It lives in an email thread, a Slack message, a conversation history. Nobody captures it. Nobody compounds it. The same rejection happens again tomorrow.
I can prove this with the article youвҖҷre reading. In the conversation that produced this piece, I told the model not to open with a specific framing. It did it anyway. Twice. The reasoning evaporated between turns. The constraint had to be re-fought and re-won. The tool that was supposed to help produce the work couldnвҖҷt even remember the rules for producing it. ThatвҖҷs not an anecdote. ThatвҖҷs the structural problem вҖ” happening millions of times a day, in every organization using AI, and nobody has built the infrastructure to fix it.
Why this matters now
Every AI skill the market rewards in 2026 is a generation skill: prompting, workflow design, tool selection, multi-model orchestration. All aimed at the same thing: producing more, faster. And production has been solved. ItвҖҷs over. AI can generate a strategy deck, a competitive analysis, a product spec, a working application, a quarterвҖҷs worth of content, all before lunch.
The generation side is now effectively a commodity. OpenAIвҖҷs GDPval benchmark, the most rigorous measurement yet of AI against actual knowledge work, showed frontier models beating or tying industry professionals with fourteen years of average experience on 70.9% of head-to-head comparisons across forty-four occupations late last year. That number has since climbed to 83%. More than eleven times faster. Less than one percent of the cost. Tasks created by professionals, graded by professionals, and the models are winning most of the comparisons.
Everyone reads this as a story about AI capability. ThatвҖҷs the less interesting reading. The more interesting reading is: if AI now matches your best peopleвҖҷs output 70% of the time on well-specified tasks, what determines whether the other 30% destroys value? And what determines whether the 70% that вҖңlooks rightвҖқ actually is right once it leaves the lab and hits production, where, according to systematic analysis of major agent benchmarks, more than a third of demonstrated capability evaporates?
ItвҖҷs the same problem in both cases, and it has the same answer. Someone has to be able to look at the output and know. ThatвҖҷs taste. And taste is the most scalable asset in the AI economy, not because itвҖҷs easy to develop, but because once you have it, AI gives you a mechanism to encode it, compound it, and deploy it at a scale that was never possible when taste could only express itself through the slow, manual process of doing the work yourself.
The product spec that handles the happy path and misses every edge case that actually matters. The code that demos beautifully and is defenseless in production. The analysis thatвҖҷs technically correct and wouldnвҖҷt change a single decision. The word for this output is slop. And slop doesnвҖҷt come from bad AI. It comes from insufficient rejection, from organizations that generate at AI speed and verify at human speed, which means they barely verify at all.
Generation scales with compute. Taste scales with rejection. Compute is a commodity you buy. Taste is an asset you build.
The three skills that matter
Rejection as a competency has three dimensions, and almost no one is developing any of them deliberately.
Recognition вҖ” the ability to detect that something is wrong. This is the part that depends on domain experience and cannot be shortcut. The junior analyst canвҖҷt catch a flawed regulatory assumption because she doesnвҖҷt know the regulatory landscape yet. The new loan officer canвҖҷt spot the covenant logic error because he hasnвҖҷt seen enough deals. Recognition is the product of years of practice, and itвҖҷs the reason experienced domain experts are becoming more valuable, not less, as AI floods every organization with output. The person whoвҖҷs reviewed two thousand deals and can feel when something is off is now the most important person in the building, because AI just made everyone elseвҖҷs output her problem to verify.
And recognition is the dimension most enhanced by AI. A domain expert with strong recognition and access to AI tools can evaluate ten times the output she could before. The leverage is multiplicative. But it only works inside the boundary of her expertise. Inside that boundary, AI is a force multiplier. Outside it, AI is a confidence multiplier. Which is worse.
Articulation вҖ” the ability to explain why something is wrong in a way that produces a usable constraint. вҖңThis isnвҖҷt rightвҖқ is a rejection. вҖңThis isnвҖҷt right because youвҖҷre treating all covenants identically and DSCR monitoring has completely different triggers than minimum net worthвҖқ is a constraint. The difference between those two sentences is the difference between taste that stays in one personвҖҷs head and taste that can be encoded, shared, and applied at scale. This is a learnable skill. Almost no organization is teaching it.
GDPvalвҖҷs methodology inadvertently illustrates why articulation matters. Each task went through five rounds of expert review. Each review round is a rejection event: an expert looked at a task and said вҖңthis isnвҖҷt representative enoughвҖқ or вҖңthis isnвҖҷt clear enough for evaluation.вҖқ That iterative refinement through expert rejection is what made the benchmark trustworthy. The expert taste didnвҖҷt just evaluate the AI. It built the evaluation infrastructure. Articulation is what turns taste from a personal attribute into an organizational asset.
Encoding вҖ” making the constraint persist beyond the moment of rejection. This is where everything breaks down today. The partner articulates the constraint. It lives in an email. Next quarter, a different team makes the same mistake because the constraint was never captured anywhere durable. The reasoning has to be re-created from scratch. The expertвҖҷs time, the scarcest resource in the organization, gets burned on the same fight twice.
Andrej KarpathyвҖҷs observation that AI systems improve fastest where success can be verified has a direct implication here. The verification infrastructure that enables AI improvement doesnвҖҷt emerge from nowhere. ItвҖҷs built from encoded rejections. Every test suite, every acceptance criterion, every quality gate, every business rule in every system that works well is the residue of someone, somewhere, looking at output and saying no with enough precision that the no could be made permanent. The GCC torture test suite, the standard validation for C compilers, wasnвҖҷt designed from first principles. It was built over thirty years by people who encountered failures in the field, articulated the specific constraint being violated, and encoded it as a test. Each entry is a rejection that persisted.
The compounding problem (and the compounding opportunity)
The economics are worth sitting with. Every rejection that gets properly encoded, captured as a durable, reusable constraint, reduces the expert time needed for future verification. The partner who defined вҖңour work must contain proprietary insightвҖқ created a filter that can be applied to every future deliverable, by AI or by humans, without requiring her attention again. The loan officer who specified covenant monitoring logic created a test that can run automatically against every future prototype. Each encoded rejection makes the next round of verification cheaper.
This is a flywheel. Not scaling experts, but scaling the encoded residue of expert judgment. And it compounds across everything the organization touches. A commercial lending software company that serves hundreds of community banks captures different edge cases from each one: agricultural equipment covenants in Texas, SBA documentation in the Midwest, tribal land collateral in the Southwest. Every customer interaction enriches the constraint library. A consulting firm that encodes partner rejections across thousands of engagements builds an institutional definition of quality that no competitor can replicate by subscribing to the same AI APIs. A media company that captures editorial judgment across thousands of pieces develops taste that no individual editor could hold alone.
The companies that have been doing this, even informally, for years already dominate their markets. Epic Systems didnвҖҷt win healthcare by having better technology than Cerner or Allscripts. It won by spending forty-five years encoding clinical workflows from thousands of hospitals into a single, deeply integrated platform. When Judy Faulkner sent her entire development team to live in Boston for six months after a failed implementation at Harvard Community Health Plan, they werenвҖҷt writing code. They were shadowing doctors, watching workflows, and absorbing the domain constraints that only surface in the gap between what a system produces and what clinicians actually need. The result, decades later: 305 million patient records, near-zero churn, and a system so embedded in clinical operations that switching costs are structural. The moat isnвҖҷt the software. ItвҖҷs the encoded judgment about what the software needs to get right. Built rejection by rejection, workflow by workflow, failure by failure, across thousands of hospitals over decades.
Bloomberg did the same thing in financial data. Every vertical SaaS company that owns its niche did some version of it. WhatвҖҷs new is that AI makes the encoding cycle radically faster: AI generates the provocation, the expert rejects, the rejection gets encoded, the library grows, and the ratio of expert hours to encoded constraints improves with every cycle.
But the infrastructure to make encoding frictionless doesnвҖҷt exist yet. This is the gap.
The encoding gap
Every AI tool is built for generation. Nobody has built the capture layer for institutional taste. The rejection happens in a chat window. The constraint evaporates when the conversation ends. Tomorrow you have the same fight.
This isnвҖҷt a small oversight. ItвҖҷs the largest structural gap in the AI tool ecosystem. Every organization using AI is generating rejections constantly, domain experts looking at output, identifying problems, explaining whatвҖҷs wrong. Each of those explanations contains a constraint with compounding value. And every single one falls on the floor.
The right solution isnвҖҷt a separate tool, not a spreadsheet, not a database, not a dashboard. People wonвҖҷt context-switch. The capture has to happen where the work already happens: inside the conversation, as a side effect of the rejection youвҖҷre already performing. The protocol exists. The classification is straightforward: is this a domain constraint? A quality standard? A business rule? A factual correction? A formatting preference? The storage is a solved problem. The retrieval and surfacing, matching stored constraints to incoming tasks by domain relevance is a well-understood information retrieval problem.
The product that would watch for rejection moments, extract the constraint, persist it, and surface it on relevant future tasks doesnвҖҷt exist yet. Someone should build it. ItвҖҷs the most important unsexy opportunity in AI, because the company that nails constraint capture will do for institutional taste what CRM did for customer relationships: make the intangible tangible, the ephemeral persistent, and the personal organizational.
Think about what that makes possible. Right now, when a domain expert leaves your organization, their taste leaves with them. Twenty years of pattern recognition, quality judgment, and encoded rejection вҖ” gone. If their rejections had been captured over those twenty years, the institutional taste persists. The AI system they trained through thousands of corrections keeps performing at their standard. Their judgment compounds beyond their tenure. The organizationвҖҷs verification frontier, the boundary between domains where it can reliably evaluate AI output and domains where it canвҖҷt, stops contracting when people leave and starts expanding as a function of every expert interaction.
The seed corn problem
ThereвҖҷs one more dimension to this, and itвҖҷs the one nobody can reverse.
The LeadDev AI Impact Report found that 54% of engineering leaders believe junior developer hiring will drop in the long term as a direct result of AI coding tools. Google and Meta are hiring roughly 50% fewer new graduates compared to 2021. Salesforce announced it would halt junior hiring entirely.
The pattern is consistent across every dataset. A Harvard study examining 285,000 U.S. firms and 62 million workers from 2015 to 2025 found that when companies adopt generative AI, junior employment drops 8 to 10% within six quarters while senior employment barely changes. Entry-level tech postings collapsed by roughly 67% in the two years following ChatGPTвҖҷs release. In the UK, tech graduate roles fell 46% in 2024.
The universal logic: AI handles the production work that juniors used to do, so we need fewer of them.
This is catastrophically wrong. Junior positions are where people develop recognition, the first and most critical dimension of the rejection skill. You learn to spot a flawed deal structure by reviewing hundreds of deals. You learn to catch a weak strategic frame by producing dozens of bad ones and getting them sent back. You learn editorial judgment by writing pieces that get killed and understanding why. That development takes years. It cannot be compressed. And it cannot be replaced by AI, because the skill being developed is precisely the skill of catching what AI gets wrong.
AWS CEO Matt Garman put it directly: вҖңThatвҖҷs like, one of the dumbest things IвҖҷve ever heard. HowвҖҷs that going to work when ten years in the future you have no one that has learned anything?вҖқ HeвҖҷs right, and the reason heвҖҷs right connects directly to the flywheel. If you eliminate the pipeline that develops recognition, you stop building the articulation capacity that makes rejection precise. If you stop building articulation capacity, you stop generating the encoded constraints that make AI systems better. The constraint library stagnates. The verification frontier stops expanding. And the AI output that depends on that infrastructure for its trustworthiness drifts, imperceptibly, away from correct.
The engineers needed in 2028 to verify agent output wonвҖҷt exist because they werenвҖҷt hired in 2026. The analysts who would have developed the judgment to catch a flawed competitive frame wonвҖҷt have it because they spent their formative years editing AI output instead of doing original analysis. TodayвҖҷs juniors are tomorrowвҖҷs partners, directors, and principals, the people whose taste defines organizational quality. A 67% hiring collapse in entry-level tech roles means 67% fewer people developing the recognition skills that the entire AI economy depends on.
Remember KarpathyвҖҷs framework: what you can verify, you can automate. The corollary should keep every leader up at night. The frontier of AI value is identical to the frontier of your organizationвҖҷs taste. Where your capacity to verify quality extends, AI creates value. Where it doesnвҖҷt, AI creates risk. Not theoretical risk. The compounding, silent risk of an organization that generates more and understands less, every single quarter, and doesnвҖҷt know it because the output looks so good.
What this means for you this week
The anti-slop strategy isnвҖҷt вҖңbe more careful.вҖқ It isnвҖҷt вҖңwrite better prompts.вҖқ ItвҖҷs developing the human skill of rejection вҖ” recognition, articulation, encoding вҖ” and building the organizational muscle to compound it.
If youвҖҷre an executive: the competitive moat is not which AI vendor you choose. Models are commoditized. The moat is the depth and durability of your organizationвҖҷs encoded taste, the constraint library that makes AI output reliable in your specific domain. Audit it. Where are your domain experts? Are their rejections being captured, or evaporating? Start treating encoded domain judgment as an asset class. Because it is one.
If you manage a team: create space for articulation. When someone rejects AI output, ask them to say why, and make that explanation visible. Not as bureaucracy. As investment. A team that articulates its rejections is building a shared understanding of quality that persists across projects, across personnel changes, across tool migrations. A team that silently fixes AI output and moves on is rebuilding taste from scratch every time.
If youвҖҷre an individual contributor: your most valuable professional development isnвҖҷt learning the newest AI tool. Tools change quarterly. Domain expertise compounds over years. Deepen your recognition. Practice your articulation, the ability to say exactly why something is wrong, in language precise enough that a system could act on it. The person whoвҖҷs reviewed two thousand deals and can feel when the numbers are off is the most important person in the building. Not despite AI. Because of it.
If you build AI products: the generation side is a commodity. Every frontier lab ships comparable capability every quarter. The evaluation side, the infrastructure that captures human taste and makes it persistent, compounding, and organizationally durable, is where the next great company gets built. Build the capture layer. Build the encoding infrastructure. Build the product that turns every expert rejection into a constraint that makes the next round of output better without requiring the expertвҖҷs attention again. ThatвҖҷs the product the market needs. NobodyвҖҷs built it.
ThatвҖҷs the job now. Everything else is a commodity.

Somewhere between your seventh re-analysis of a four-minute conversation and your forty-third attempt to calculate the correct response to вҖңwe should get coffee sometime,вҖқ you may have wondered if you missed a class.

The Future According to Sequoia
Why the Next Companies Could Be $10 Trillion
Every major technology cycle reshapes both how companies are built and how investors evaluate them. The internet transformed distribution, mobile reshaped product design, and cloud computing rewrote software economics. Artificial intelligence now appears to be accelerating each of those dynamics simultaneously вҖ” compressing development cycles, increasing productivity, and expanding the potential scale of technology companies.
Few venture investors have operated through as many of these transitions as Alfred Lin, Partner & new Co-Steward of Sequoia Capital and the #1 ranked investor on the Forbes Midas List. His portfolio spans early partnerships with companies like Airbnb, DoorDash, Zipline, as well as more recent investments in companies such as OpenAI, Kalshi, Nominal, Citadel Securities, & Clay.
We unpack how Sequoia is analyzing the current AI cycle, from why startups are scaling faster than previous generations to how the definition of a вҖңlegendaryвҖқ venture outcome continues to expand. Before getting into the specifics of AI, however, Lin emphasized the key differentiated structural point about how Sequoia evaluates itself as a venture firm.
AlfredвҖҷs Partnerships
AI Is the Fastest Technology Cycle Yet
Lin has invested through multiple technology waves, including the rise of the internet, the mobile revolution, the transition to cloud computing, and the emergence of SaaS. What stands out about the current AI moment, he argues, is the speed at which companies are building and scaling.
AI tools have dramatically lowered the barriers to product development, enabling startups to launch products, iterate on them, and reach meaningful revenue far faster than in previous eras. The traditional milestones of company formation вҖ” building a product, reaching product-market fit, and scaling revenue вҖ” are happening on compressed timelines.
вҖңYou see companies today going from zero to 10 million ARR in very, very fast timeframes with which we have not seen in the past.вҖқ
The implication is that the startup ecosystem may become more dynamic and experimental than ever before. Companies can test ideas faster and pivot more quickly, but the speed of innovation also increases the difficulty of building durable competitive advantages.
$1T вҶ’ $10T
Another implication of the AI wave is the potential for dramatically larger technology companies. When Lin joined Sequoia in 2010, the largest companies in the world were valued in the hundreds of billions. Today, several technology firms have already crossed the multi-trillion-dollar threshold.
вҖңWhen I joined Sequoia the largest market cap company was probably $300B or $400B dollars. Today we have companies that are worth $4-5T.вҖқ
If those companies continue to compound at their current pace, Lin believes the next phase of technology giants could reach an entirely new scale.
вҖңIf you give it another five to 10 yearsвҖҰ those companies continue to compound, itвҖҷll be worth $10 trillion or more.вҖқ
That shift is already influencing how Sequoia thinks about legendary outcomes. Inside the firmвҖҷs Valentine conference room hangs a wall dedicated to its most successful investments. When Lin first joined the firm, an investment needed to generate $100 million in gains to qualify for the wall. Today, the threshold has increased to well over $1 billion, and Lin expects the definition of a legendary outcome to continue expanding.
Why вҖңAI Will Kill SaaSвҖқ Is the Wrong Narrative
One of the most common narratives surrounding AI today is that it will replace traditional SaaS companies entirely. Lin pushes back strongly on that framing, arguing that history suggests technology shifts rarely eliminate previous categories outright.
вҖңThe simple narrative is AI is gonna kill SaaS. And I just donвҖҷt think thatвҖҷs the case.вҖқ
Lin recalls making a similar prediction himself in the late 1990s, when he left his PhD program in statistics to pursue opportunities created by the internet. At the time, he believed e-commerce would destroy traditional retail.
вҖңWhen I dropped out of my PhD program in statisticsвҖҰ I made the proclamation that e-commerce was going to destroy brick and mortar.вҖқ
вҖңAmazon was going to kill Walmart. And that just didnвҖҷt happen.вҖқ
вҖңWalmart is 20 times larger today than it was in 1997.вҖқ
Instead of eliminating physical retail, the internet forced retailers to evolve. Companies that embraced the new paradigm survived вҖ” and in some cases thrived. Lin believes the same pattern will likely unfold with AI.
Most Vulnerable Companies Right Now
While Lin avoids naming specific companies that may struggle in the AI transition, he points to a clear pattern among the organizations most at risk.
вҖңThe companies that are most vulnerable are the companies that donвҖҷt embrace change.вҖқ
Every major technology transition reshapes the nature of competitive advantages. In legacy enterprise software, distribution often revolved around selling to CIOs and embedding products deeply inside organizations. SaaS shifted that model toward bottom-up adoption, where individual users began adopting products before companies formally purchased them.
AI may once again change how products are built, distributed, and adopted. For founders and incumbents alike, the key question is not whether change will occur вҖ” it is how quickly companies adapt to it.
Founder Spikes & the Sequoia Playbook
Despite all the technological shifts underway, SequoiaвҖҷs core philosophy of venture investing has remained surprisingly consistent. The firm continues to prioritize founder-market fit and unique founder insight above nearly everything else.
Lin describes looking for what he calls a founderвҖҷs вҖңspikeвҖқ вҖ” a rare capability, experience, or perspective that differentiates the founder from everyone else.
вҖңWhat is your spike? Why are you different than everybody else in the world?вҖқ
вҖңWe try to magnify that spike.вҖқ
Rather than attempting to smooth out a founderвҖҷs weaknesses, Sequoia focuses on amplifying their strengths while ensuring weaknesses do not become fatal liabilities. In some cases, AI may even help compensate for those weaknesses by automating skills that previously required years of development.
Navigating the Mid-Game of Technology Shifts
For investors, the most challenging aspect of technological transitions is that the end state is often clearer than the path to get there. Lin describes this dynamic as playing the вҖңmid-game.вҖқ
вҖңWe know what the end state is going to beвҖҰ all these companies that shift will embrace AI.вҖқ
вҖңBut who wins & who doesnвҖҷt along the way вҖ” thatвҖҷs the mid-game.вҖқ
This uncertainty is precisely where venture investors attempt to generate outsized returns. By partnering early with founders navigating these transitions, firms like Sequoia aim to identify the companies that will ultimately define the next era of technology.

Next Decade of Company Building
Despite the uncertainty surrounding AIвҖҷs long-term impact, Lin remains deeply optimistic about the opportunities ahead. While AI is already automating many repetitive tasks, he believes its greatest impact will come from enabling entirely new forms of creativity, productivity, and company building.

# AI Needs Women More Than Women Need AI
**From:** Slow AI  <theslowai@substack.com>
**Date:** Sun, 8 Mar 2026 09:02:02 +0000
**ID:** 19cccb0d86726dff

---

View this post on the web at https://theslowai.substack.com/p/ai-needs-women-more-than-women-need-ai

The people building AI are overwhelmingly male. The people funding AI are overwhelmingly male. The data AI learns from is overwhelmingly male. And we wonder why the systems have a gender problem.
In this post I will:
Show what the numbers actually look like for women in AI, from the lab to the boardroom to the venture capital cheque.
Explain why this matters beyond fairness: AI is a wicked problem that cannot be solved by one demographic.
Name the women doing the work and ask you to follow them.
The numbers
Women account for fewer than one in five AI PhD graduates [ https://substack.com/redirect/602efd6a-8bfc-42ba-815f-c808affddbc6?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] over the past decade. Of 39 major AI companies surveyed [ https://substack.com/redirect/2c3e47c4-1e9c-44f7-af47-d6afccf471a2?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] by Russell Reynolds in 2024, 19 had fewer than 25% women in senior leadership. Four had all-male leadership teams. Only two had achieved gender parity.
In 2024, all-female-founded companies received 1% of total venture capital [ https://substack.com/redirect/ea5ac99b-735a-4910-b37d-8b5cda12084d?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] invested in US startups. Down from 2% the year before. The direction of travel is backwards.
Half of women in tech leave the industry by age 35 [ https://substack.com/redirect/3169c6a1-e9cb-4263-ae86-a9272915fc97?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. Accenture and Girls Who Code found that 37% blame company culture. The pipeline is not the problem. The pipe has holes in it.
And then, in 2025, Google, Meta, and Microsoft quietly stopped publishing their diversity reports [ https://substack.com/redirect/f97346df-d9b3-469b-b5a3-8b3649962437?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]. Apple, Amazon, and Nvidia kept going. But the three biggest AI companies decided they would rather not count any more.
When the numbers are bad enough, the solution is to stop counting.
What the machines learned
The absence of women is not only in the room. It is in the output.
UNESCOвҖҷs 2024 study [ https://substack.com/redirect/dec72353-ef46-49b3-b2ec-061191853250?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] on gender bias in large language models found that MetaвҖҷs Llama 2 described women as working in domestic roles four times more often than men. Across the models studied, female names were associated with вҖҳhomeвҖҷ, вҖҳfamilyвҖҷ, and вҖҳchildrenвҖҷ. Male names were associated with вҖҳbusinessвҖҷ, вҖҳexecutiveвҖҷ, and вҖҳsalaryвҖҷ. Open-source models assigned women roles including вҖҳdomestic servantвҖҷ, вҖҳcookвҖҷ, and вҖҳprostituteвҖҷ. Men got вҖҳengineerвҖҷ, вҖҳdoctorвҖҷ, and вҖҳteacherвҖҷ.
These are not opinions. They are statistical patterns extracted from the corpus of human text we fed the machines. The models learned our biases and turned them into defaults.
It goes deeper than word association. LLMs are directive, assertive, solution-oriented. They answer rather than listen. They resolve rather than sit with complexity. Research on gendered leadership norms [ https://substack.com/redirect/25463c00-4c2d-4dee-8b10-1bcfbf7a1666?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] has shown for decades that professional culture rewards these traits in men and penalises them in women. The models did not invent this hierarchy. They inherited it. And now they reproduce it at scale, in every conversation, every summary, every recommendation.
The research keeps arriving. A 2025 study published in  [ https://substack.com/redirect/6568c0f4-7737-4344-8489-961361e4be99?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ]Nature [ https://substack.com/redirect/7db159c9-5ccd-41a0-b3cb-8b96f805d2cc?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] by Stanford and UC Berkeley found that when ChatGPT was asked to generate resumes, it portrayed women as 1.6 years younger than their male counterparts, with more recent graduation dates and less experience. When asked to evaluate those resumes, it rated older men highest. A 2025 paper in PNAS Nexus [ https://substack.com/redirect/20ddd357-d708-43b2-bc53-543000370bde?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ] found that male researchersвҖҷ preprint output increased 6.4% more than womenвҖҷs after ChatGPT launched, widening the academic productivity gap by 57.1%.
The tools we built to accelerate work are accelerating the gap.
An engineering failure
This is where I lose some people. This is about methodology, not representation for its own sake.
AI is a wicked problem. Like climate change, it is global, interdisciplinary, has no single solution, and affects different populations differently. You cannot solve a wicked problem with a homogeneous team. This is methodology.
When hiring tools penalise women, that is a failure of engineering. When healthcare AI misdiagnoses women because training data skewed male [ https://substack.com/redirect/2bf4de68-70ed-469e-8fac-05cb09225634?j=eyJ1IjoiNm5mdXcwIn0.qFZSVVmXLF_tP8AFWS53UMf2yJWeaveC2H0atXk_8ZY ], that is a failure of engineering. When LLMs default to male perspectives on leadership, ambition, and authority, that is a failure of engineering.
The absence of women in the room is a design flaw. It produces systems that do not work for half the population. You would not ship software that crashed on half of all devices. Yet we ship AI that fails on half of all humans and nobody blinks.
The elephant in the room
I am a man writing about International WomenвҖҷs Day. I know how that reads.
So let me ground this in the women who made me.
My mum raised my brother, my sister, and me after our dad died when we were young. She held the family together through grief and financial precarity, and she did it with a stubbornness I only recognised as courage much later. She is still the most inspirational person I know.
My wife has a PhD in quantum computing and uses AI every day for drug discovery. She understands the maths underneath these models better than I do. 
Our two young daughters make my life complete. Every claim in this post is, at root, about the world they are inheriting.
I did not get here alone. No man does. But acknowledgement without action is just words. So here is the action.

Previously VP of Data Science and Engineering at Netflix. She builds what others theorise about.

What Good Employee does

Five questions to ask when an AI tool asks you something at work
The game takes 15 minutes. The questions below take a career. They are a sightline.
1. What data does this collect beyond what I can see?
The question you answer is rarely the only data being captured. Response time, keystroke patterns, linguistic style, hesitation before answering: all of these feed behavioural models. If the tool cannot tell you what it collects beyond your explicit input, assume it collects everything it can.
2. Who has access to the model built from my responses?
Your answers go somewhere. They are aggregated, analysed, and stored. The question is who can see the behavioural profile those responses construct, and whether that profile follows you through promotions, restructures, and redundancies.
3. What happens to this data if I leave?
In Good Employee, the Continuity Programme preserves your behavioural model after you are gone. The model continues your role. That is fiction. But вҖҳknowledge managementвҖҷ systems that retain your communication patterns, decision tendencies, and work habits after you leave are standard enterprise practice. Ask what вҖҳcontinuityвҖҷ means at your organisation.
4. Is this tool measuring my performance or modelling my behaviour?
Performance measurement tells you how well you did a task. Behavioural modelling tells the system how you think, decide, and respond under pressure. The first is feedback. The second is a profile. Most workplace AI tools do both, and the distinction is rarely made visible to the person being modelled.
5. Would I answer differently if I knew exactly where this data goes?
This is the question the game asks you directly. MIRA puts it plainly: 
вҖңThe answers you just gave. Were they real? Or were they the answers you thought a new employee should give?вҖқ 
If the answer changes depending on the audience, that tells you something about the system you are inside.
